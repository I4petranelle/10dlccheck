CTIA Messaging Guidelines Explained

A guide to CTIA Messaging Principles and Best Practices (May 2023)

On this page

    Introduction and Purpose

    The CTIA Messaging Principles and Best Practices framework promotes trusted wireless messaging, protects consumers, and clarifies expectations for all participants in the messaging ecosystem.

    • Encourage trusted and effective wireless messaging, including SMS, MMS, and RCS.
    • Protect consumers from unwanted or fraudulent content.
    • Provide clarity to senders, providers, and carriers.
    • Complement but not replace legal obligations under TCPA, CAN-SPAM, and FCC rules.

    These guidelines primarily cover messaging using 10-digit long codes (10DLC) and short codes. They apply to message senders, aggregators, carriers, and service providers operating within the U.S. wireless ecosystem.

    Key Concepts and Roles

    The CTIA distinguishes between Consumer (P2P) and Non-Consumer (A2P) messaging. Business and organizational messaging is considered Non-Consumer and held to stricter consent, content, and privacy standards.

    Consumer Messaging

    Messaging between individuals, conversational and lower volume, maintaining balanced send and receive ratios.

    Non-Consumer Messaging

    Business or organizational messaging to consumers, such as marketing, alerts, or notifications. These require clear consent, opt-outs, and transparency.

    Non-Consumer Best Practices

    Consumer Consent

    Consent is the foundation of compliance. Senders must obtain, document, and honor consumer opt-ins.

    Example CTA: “Text JOIN123 to 55555 to receive weekly offers from YourCompany. Msg and data rates may apply. Up to 4 msgs per week. Reply STOP to cancel.”
    • Opt-ins must be explicit and campaign-specific.
    • Confirmation messages must include sender name, opt-out information, frequency, and cost.
    • Opt-outs must be simple and immediate using STOP, UNSUBSCRIBE, or CANCEL.
    • Do not rent, buy, or share consent lists.

    Privacy and Security

    Maintain a clear privacy policy and strong security controls for access, encryption, and audits. Remove deactivated numbers promptly.

    Content Requirements

    • No deceptive, fraudulent, or illegal content.
    • Links must identify the sender, and public link shorteners are discouraged.
    • Phone numbers within messages must identify the associated entity.

    Text-Enabling a Telephone Number

    Senders must validate authority to use a 10-digit number for messaging. It should be properly assigned through a telecom provider.

    Other Practices

    • Avoid “snowshoe” tactics that distribute traffic over many numbers to evade filters.
    • Do not use unauthorized or “grey” routes.
    • Monitor proxy or shared numbers closely for abuse.

    Special Use Cases

    The CTIA also provides guidance for group messaging, number spoofing, and registries, requiring fairness, transparency, and opt-out support in all implementations.

    Mitigating Unwanted Messaging

    • Providers should proactively block or filter unwanted messages.
    • Consumers can report spam using the 7726 (“SPAM”) shortcode.
    • Bad actors may be suspended or blacklisted.

    Carrier and Industry Implications

    • Carriers may filter traffic based on opt-in status, reputation, or complaint rates.
    • Non-compliant senders risk message blocking or throttling.
    • Maintain detailed opt-in and opt-out logs for audits.

    Example Scenarios

    Promotional Campaign

    “Text OFFERS to 12345 to receive deals from ShopCo. Up to 3 msgs per week. Reply STOP to unsubscribe.”

    Send confirmation, log consent, and include STOP in all subsequent messages.

    Informational Alerts

    “Alert: Unusual login attempt detected. Reply YES if you recognize it.”

    Ensure branding, domain transparency, and opt-out options for non-essential messaging.

    Legal Context

    CTIA guidelines are voluntary and not laws. Compliance with TCPA, CAN-SPAM, FCC, and state laws remains mandatory. Carriers may impose stricter internal rules.

    Implementation Tips

    • Integrate compliance early in your workflow.
    • Use dedicated numbers to isolate risk.
    • Monitor opt-outs, bounces, and complaint rates.
    • Review your messaging strategy quarterly to align with CTIA updates.
